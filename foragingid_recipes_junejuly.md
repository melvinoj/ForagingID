Title: Melvin's Sweet Chestnut Coffee with Warm Spiced Chestnut Pudding

A symphony of autumnal flavors, this recipe celebrates the richness and depth of Castanea sativa, also known as Sweet Chestnuts. The seed's sweet potato-like taste, enhanced by roasting, forms the backbone of both our coffee substitute and luscious chestnut pudding.

**Ingredients:**
1. 500g fresh Sweet Chestnuts
2. 1 litre water (for brewing coffee)
3. 1 large orange
4. 4 cinnamon sticks
5. 10 cloves
6. 5 star anise
7. 2 tbsp honey or maple syrup
8. 1 vanilla pod, split
9. Coconut cream (for serving)
10. Sea salt flakes
11. Fresh mint leaves (for garnish)

**Method:**

*Preparing the Chestnuts:*
Roast the chestnuts in a preheated oven at 200°C for about 30 minutes, or until the outer shells are crisp and darkened. Allow them to cool before peeling off the shells and the inner brown skin. Set aside half of the roasted chestnuts for the pudding.

*Brewing the Coffee:*
Place the remaining chestnuts in a large pot with water, orange zest, cinnamon sticks, cloves, star anise, honey, vanilla pod, and a generous pinch of sea salt. Simmer gently for about 45 minutes until the chestnuts are soft. Strain the mixture through a fine-mesh sieve, reserving the liquid. The chestnut-infused water can be used as a coffee substitute. Serve hot with a dollop of coconut cream and a sprinkle of sea salt for an exhilarating kick.

*Preparing the Warm Spiced Chestnut Pudding:*
In a large saucepan, combine the reserved roasted chestnuts, half of the infused water (or substitute regular water), and honey or maple syrup. Add the cinnamon sticks, cloves, star anise, vanilla pod, and a pinch of sea salt. Simmer gently for about 30 minutes, until the mixture thickens to a pudding-like consistency. Serve warm with the chestnut coffee.

**Variation or Pairing:**
For an extra indulgent touch, top the pudding with a scoop of spiced chestnut ice cream. Enjoy this warming dessert with a glass of sweet chestnut wine, perfect for those crisp autumn evenings.

**Recipe: Melvin's Mountain Sow Thistle & Wild Garlic Risotto**

In the highlands of Sheffield, where nature weaves her tapestry of wild edibles, there lies a humble yet vibrant find - the Mountain Sow Thistle (Cicerbita alpina). Its tall, blue-lettuce-like leaves whisper a subtle aniseed note that dances beautifully with the earthy undertones of wild garlic. This dish is a testament to their marriage in a heartwarming symphony of flavors.

**Ingredients:**
1. Fresh Mountain Sow Thistle shoots and stems - peeled and sliced (approximately 3 cups)
2. Wild Garlic bulbs, leaves, and flowers - finely chopped (about 2 cups)
3. Arborio rice (1.5 cups)
4. White wine (1 cup)
5. Vegetable broth (6 cups)
6. Olive oil (2 tbsp)
7. Salt (to taste)
8. Freshly ground black pepper (to taste)
9. Grated Parmesan cheese (1/2 cup, plus extra for garnish)
10. Unsalted butter (4 tbsp)

**Method:**

1. Begin by heating the vegetable broth in a saucepan and keeping it warm on low heat.

2. In another large pan, sauté the peeled Mountain Sow Thistle slices in olive oil until they soften, about 5 minutes. Remove them from the pan and set aside.

3. Add the wild garlic to the same pan and cook until fragrant, around 2-3 minutes. Do not let it brown.

4. Stir in the Arborio rice, allowing it to absorb the flavors of the garlic and oil.

5. Pour in the white wine, letting it simmer until fully absorbed by the rice.

6. Gradually add warm vegetable broth to the rice, one ladle at a time, stirring continuously until each addition is fully absorbed before adding more. This process should take about 20-25 minutes.

7. Once the rice is cooked al dente, stir in the Mountain Sow Thistle, wild garlic, butter, salt, and pepper. Let it cook for another 2 minutes to meld the flavors.

8. Remove from heat and fold in the grated Parmesan cheese.

9. Serve immediately with a dusting of fresh Parmesan cheese and edible wild garlic flowers as garnish.

**Variation or Pairing:**
For an exotic twist, try swapping out the white wine for a Mexican Pasilla chile-infused tequila to give the dish a smoky kick. Enjoy this risotto with locally sourced, wood-roasted game meats, or let it shine on its own as a plant-based feast that honors our earth's bountiful offerings.

## Ajuga Shoots with Charred Onion, Walnut, and Black Garlic

# Ajuga Shoots with Charred Onion, Walnut, and Black Garlic

Ajuga grows wild across Sheffield and most of temperate Europe—low, creeping, persistent. The young shoots arrive in early spring with an almost mineral bitterness, clean herbaceous snap, and a faint nuttiness that sits somewhere between watercress and young horehound. It's a plant that tastes like it's paying attention. I use it raw mostly, occasionally warmed through briefly, because heat flattens what makes it interesting: that bright, almost astringent edge that cuts through fat and anchors a plate.

**Ingredients**

400g ajuga shoots, young and tender (roughly the top 6–8cm of growth)  
3 medium onions, cut into thick wedges  
80g walnuts, roughly broken  
4 cloves black garlic, sliced thin  
3 tbsp good olive oil  
1 tbsp red wine vinegar  
1 tsp Maldon salt  
Black pepper  
A squeeze of lemon (optional)

**Method**

Get your pan very hot—cast iron is best. Toss the onion wedges with a tablespoon of oil and a pinch of salt, then lay them flat in the pan and leave them. Don't move them. You want real colour, almost scorched in places. This takes 8–10 minutes. The onion should be soft inside, bitter and sweet on the outside. Transfer to a bowl.

While that's happening, gently wash and pat the ajuga shoots dry. Check them over—discard any that look tired or bruised. They should feel fresh and slightly springy.

Toast the walnuts in a dry pan for 3 minutes, just until they smell alive. Add them to the onions.

Make your dressing: whisk together the remaining olive oil, the vinegar, salt, and a turn of black pepper. Taste it. It should be sharp and clean, not shy.

Just before you serve, toss the ajuga shoots gently through the warm onion and walnut mixture. Add the black garlic. Pour the dressing over everything and toss loosely—you want the shoots to keep some of their structure and that raw brightness. Let it sit for 2 minutes so the heat just kissed them and the dressing settles.

Plate it generously, letting things overlap. The bitterness of the ajuga against the caramelised onion sweetness and the umami depth of black garlic is where this works. Serve at room temperature.

**Variation**

In late spring, when ajuga starts to flower, the purple flower spikes are edible too—use them whole, raw, scattered on top as both texture and colour. Or pair this with soft young goat cheese and a handful of toasted seeds (pumpkin, sunflower) for more weight and body.

## Aquilegia Vulgaris Salad with Whipped Labneh, Cucumber Water, and Toasted Dukkah

# Aquilegia Vulgaris Salad with Whipped Labneh, Cucumber Water, and Toasted Dukkah

---

Columbine flowers are one of the quiet surprises of early summer — visually dramatic, almost architectural in how they hold themselves, but when you actually eat one there's this clean, honeyed sweetness drawn from the nectar at the base of those curved spurs. That sweetness is what this dish is built around — it needs contrast, acidity, and something savoury and substantial underneath to carry it. Whipped labneh and a sharp herb oil give the flower somewhere real to land.

---

**Serves 4 as a starter or side**

**For the whipped labneh:**
500g full-fat labneh
2 tbsp good olive oil
Zest of half a lemon
1 small clove garlic, grated fine
Pinch of fine sea salt

**For the cucumber water:**
1 large cucumber
Juice of half a lemon
Pinch of salt
Small handful of fresh mint leaves

**For the dukkah:**
50g hazelnuts, toasted and roughly crushed
2 tbsp sesame seeds, toasted
1 tsp coriander seed, dry-toasted and cracked
½ tsp cumin seed, dry-toasted and cracked
½ tsp dried thyme
Flaked sea salt and black pepper

**For the dish:**
30–40 Aquilegia vulgaris flowers, freshly picked — a mix of colours if you have them
Small handful of pea shoots or purslane if available
Finishing olive oil, the best you have
A few drops of good red wine vinegar

---

**Method**

Start with the labneh. Tip it into a bowl, add the olive oil, lemon zest, garlic, and salt, then whip it with a spatula or in a stand mixer until it lightens slightly in texture — you want it spreadable and silky, not stiff. Taste it. It should have brightness from the lemon, a very faint warmth from the garlic, and enough salt to carry whatever goes on top of it. Set it aside at room temperature while you build everything else.

For the cucumber water, peel the cucumber roughly — you don't need to be precise — then blend it with the lemon juice, salt, and mint. Push it through a fine sieve or muslin. What you want is a pale, clear green liquid that tastes properly of cucumber, with just enough sharpness to cut through fat. If you haven't done this before, it will surprise you how much flavour is there. Keep it cold.

The dukkah is a loose version — more textured than powder. Combine the crushed hazelnuts with the sesame seeds and cracked spices, the thyme, and a good pinch of flaked salt. Mix it with your hands. It shouldn't be uniform — some pieces larger, some finer. That inconsistency is the point.

To assemble, take a wide, shallow bowl or a flat plate. Spoon a generous pool of the whipped labneh towards the centre and spread it out with the back of a spoon, leaving the edges uneven and natural. Pour two or three tablespoons of the cold cucumber water into the bowl around the labneh — not on top of it, alongside it, so the two remain distinct. Scatter the dukkah across the labneh, not the cucumber water. Now lay the columbine flowers over the top, handling them as gently as you can — they're delicate and they bruise. Tuck in any pea shoots or purslane if you have them. Finish with a thin thread of olive oil over everything and two or three drops of red wine vinegar landed directly on the flowers.

Eat it immediately. The flowers are at their best the moment they're dressed — they'll wilt within minutes and the dish loses its lift.

## Barbarea Vulgaris with Anchovy, Lemon & Burnt Breadcrumb

# Barbarea Vulgaris with Anchovy, Lemon & Burnt Breadcrumb

Wintercress arrives when you need it most — a muscular, peppery green that holds its structure through cooking and tastes genuinely alive even in February. The young leaves carry that mustard heat without becoming harsh, and the buds, if you catch them before they flower, cook down to something almost broccoli-like. This is a dish about contrast: the sharp greens against anchovy's umami depth, lemon's clarity cutting through the slight bitterness, and burnt breadcrumb for texture and soul.

**Serves 4**

500g young wintercress leaves and tender stems (picked from clean ground, away from roads)
6 good anchovy fillets, rinsed
2 cloves garlic, thinly sliced
80ml olive oil
60g stale bread, torn into uneven pieces
1 lemon, halved
Fleur de sel
Black pepper

**Method**

Bring a large pot of salted water to a rolling boil. While it heats, make your burnt breadcrumb. Put a heavy pan over medium-high heat with 40ml of the olive oil. When it's moving, add the torn bread pieces — they should sizzle immediately. Move them constantly with a wooden spoon, breaking them down as they colour. You want deep gold bleeding into copper, some almost black. This takes 6 or 7 minutes. The smell will shift from toasted to almost smoky. Scrape onto paper towel, season with salt.

Drop your wintercress into the boiling water. Don't walk away. The young leaves will soften in 2 to 3 minutes — that's what you're after. The stems might need another minute. Taste a leaf; it should yield completely but still taste green, with that peppery note held underneath rather than screaming at you. Drain it all, pressing gently with the back of a spoon to release water without crushing.

Whitebeam fruit spent half the year overlooked in hedgerows across northern Europe—small, hard, forbidding—until someone discovered that time and patience could transform it into something almost tropical. Fleur de sel

## Brown Butter Sage with White Beans, Preserved Lemon & Toasted Walnuts

Sage is one of those plants that asks you to respect it. Too much heat and it turns acrid — but find the right moment, the exact second the butter foams and the leaf crisps without burning, and it transforms into something almost meaty, resinous, deeply aromatic. ## Burdock Root with Black Garlic, Preserved Lemon & Toasted Sesame

# Burdock Root with Black Garlic, Preserved Lemon & Toasted Sesame

---

Burdock is one of those roots that rewards patience and a generous hand with seasoning — left to its own devices it's mild, almost blank, but that quality is exactly what makes it exceptional. It absorbs flavour the way good bread absorbs oil, drawing it inward rather than just wearing it on the surface. In Japan they've known this for centuries with gobō; here I'm pulling it toward something North African in spirit, using black garlic and preserved lemon to do the heavy lifting.

---

**Serves 4 as a side or starter**

- 600g fresh burdock root, harvested young (no longer than 60cm, ideally), scrubbed well
- 6 cloves black garlic, peeled
- 1 preserved lemon, flesh discarded, rind finely chopped
- 3 tbsp good sesame oil
- 2 tbsp light soy sauce or tamari
- 1 tbsp apple cider vinegar
- 1 tsp honey or date syrup
- 2 tbsp toasted sesame seeds
- Small handful of flat-leaf parsley, roughly torn
- Cold water with a squeeze of lemon juice, for holding the cut root
- Flaked salt and cracked black pepper to finish

---

**Method**

Start by preparing your burdock as soon as it comes out of the ground or as soon as you've sourced it — the root discolours quickly once cut, so have a bowl of cold acidulated water ready before you pick up the knife. Scrub the root firmly but don't peel it; the skin holds flavour and texture, and on a young root there's no need to remove it. Cut into batons roughly 6–7cm long and 1cm wide, dropping each piece straight into the lemon water as you go.

Bring a large pan of well-salted water to a rolling boil. Drain the burdock, add it to the boiling water and cook for around 12–15 minutes. You're looking for tender but with genuine resistance still — not soft, not squeaky. Taste one at the 10-minute mark. Drain and let it steam-dry briefly on a clean cloth.

While the root cooks, make your dressing. Crush the black garlic to a paste — a knife and a little patience will do it, or a mortar if you want control over the texture. Combine with the chopped preserved lemon rind, sesame oil, soy, vinegar, and honey. Whisk it together until it comes into itself. Taste it. It should have depth, a low saltiness, a little sweet and sour brightness. Adjust accordingly — this is where your judgement matters more than mine.

Heat a wide pan or skillet over a high flame with just a thin film of neutral oil. When it's properly hot, add the drained burdock in a single layer and leave it alone for 2–3 minutes. You want some colour on it, some caramelisation at the edges. Toss and repeat briefly on the other side. Remove from the heat, pour the dressing directly into the hot pan over the burdock, and toss everything together so the root drinks it in while it's still warm and open.

Transfer to a serving plate, scatter over the toasted sesame seeds and the torn parsley, finish with flaked salt and a little cracked pepper. Serve warm or at room temperature — it's honestly better rested for 10 minutes.

---

**To vary:** If you're cooking this in late spring when the young stems are pushing up, peel and parboil the petioles alongside the root — they bring an asparagus-like quality that stretches the dish into something more complex. A spoonful of white miso stirred into the dressing in place of some of the soy will take it somewhere quieter and deeper still.

## Caramelised Onion Broth with Slow-Roasted Allium & Foraged Herbs

# Caramelised Onion Broth with Slow-Roasted Allium & Foraged Herbs

There's a sweetness in an onion that most people never meet. They know it cooked, sure — but they've not sat with it, let it go through its stages, watched it move from sharp and aggressive through to something almost honeyed, with a depth that sits right at the back of the throat. This recipe treats the onion as what it is: a bulb capable of transformation, and a broth that carries everything else.

## Ingredients

- 6 large onions (sweet varieties if you can find them — Spanish or Roscoff)
- 2 smaller onions, kept whole in their skins
- 3 spring onions, roots intact
- 1.5 litres good vegetable stock, preferably homemade
- Olive oil
- Salt, flaky sea salt
- Fresh thyme (foraged if you have it — wild thyme has more bite)
- Wild garlic leaves, a handful (or three-cornered leek if it's in season)
- Wild fennel pollen, if you can get it
- Black pepper
- A splash of cider vinegar or verjuice

## Method

Get your oven to 160°C. Take three of those big onions — leave the skins on — and put them whole into a roasting dish. Drizzle with oil, a good pinch of salt, and roast them for an hour and a half. You want them soft right through, the skins papery and charred in places, the flesh giving when you press it. When they're cool enough to handle, peel them, chop them roughly, and set aside with their juices.

The other three onions — peel them, slice them thin, half-moons. Get a heavy-bottomed pan, put a slick of oil in it, medium heat. Add the sliced onions and a good pinch of salt. Stir. Then don't touch them for five minutes. You want them to catch, just a little, before you stir again. Keep doing this — patience now pays later. After about twenty-five minutes, they'll be deep amber, almost mahogany in places, sticky and sweet. Deglaze with a splash of water or stock, scrape up the bits, then add the rest of the stock, the roasted onion flesh and juices, and the two whole onions in their skins.

Simmer gently for thirty minutes. Taste it. You're building a broth here, not a soup — it should be clear in intent if not in appearance. The whole onions in their skins will have given up their deepest flavours, and you'll fish them out before serving. Strain the broth through a fine sieve, pressing down on the solids, then return to the pan.

Finely slice the spring onions, whites and greens separate. Chop the wild garlic leaves into ribbons. Taste the broth again — adjust with salt, a crack of pepper, that small splash of vinegar to lift it.

Ladle into bowls. Top with the sliced spring onion whites, then the greens, then the wild garlic. Finish with a pinch of wild fennel pollen if you have it, and a thread of good olive oil. Serve with a hunk of sourdough, or — if you want to make it a meal — a poached egg dropped in just before serving, the yolk running into the broth.

## To vary it

Swap the wild garlic for whatever allium is about — chive flowers in late spring, the green seed heads of wild garlic in early summer. And if you've got a piece of smoked cheese to grate over the top, that's not a bad idea either.

# Charred Marsh Thistle Stems with Anchovy, Burnt Garlic & Wild Thyme

Marsh thistle is one of those plants that teaches you something the moment you taste it. The stems have a genuine sweetness — celery-like, but greener, less hollow — with a mineral backbone that speaks of wet ground. Peel away the spines and you find something tender worth the small effort. This is spring work, when the stems are still full of promise before the plant hardens into flower.

**Serves 4 as a side or part of something bigger**

Marsh thistle stems (enough to fill your hand — roughly 400g), the young ones, cut from near the base  
4 anchovy fillets  
3 cloves of garlic, unpeeled

Black pepper  
A small handful of wild thyme or oregano, whatever grows near you

---

**Method**

## Cleavers & Burnt Onion Broth with Roasted Seed Coffee

# Cleavers & Burnt Onion Broth with Roasted Seed Coffee

Cleavers arrives in spring with an almost mineral bitterness—the kind that wakes your palate up. It's too sharp to eat raw in quantity, but cooked briefly and folded into broth, it becomes something else entirely: grassy, green, alive. The roasted seeds make a surprisingly good coffee substitute that I've used for years when I want something warm without caffeine. This recipe sits at the intersection of both—a simple broth that knows what it's doing.

**For the broth:**
2 large onions, halved
500ml good vegetable stock or water
A good fistful of fresh cleavers shoots (the tender tips)

Sea salt, black pepper
Olive oil

**For the coffee:**
Cleavers seeds, collected and dried

The bitterness will still be there—that's the point—but it'll be green and alive rather than acrid.

Grind them coarsely and brew like coffee. The bitterness carries through—it's not a perfect replica, but it's honest and warming.

# Corn Mint Broth with Early Greens and Burnt Alliums

**Method**

**Method**

Start with the marrow. Preheat your oven to 220°C. Stand the bones upright on a baking tray if you can — it holds the fat better — and roast for 15 to 18 minutes until the marrow is softened, slightly translucent, and just beginning to pull away from the bone. While it's still hot, scoop the marrow out into a wide, heavy pan. Don't waste what clings — use a small spoon and be deliberate about it. This fat is the foundation of everything.

Set the pan over a medium heat. When the marrow starts to melt and shimmer, add your red onion and the crushed garlic. Let them go slow — 10 minutes at least, stirring occasionally. You want softness and sweetness here, not colour. The chilli goes in now too, just to warm through and bloom in the fat.

Add the elderberries. If you're using fresh, they'll start to burst almost immediately. If dried and rehydrated, they'll take a few minutes longer and give you a slightly rounder, less bitter flavour — either is good, just different. Stir them in and let them sit in the heat for a few minutes before you do anything else. You want them to begin breaking down before the liquid arrives.

Pour in the red wine. It'll spit and steam — let it. Add the vinegar and honey and tuck in the thyme sprigs. Bring it up to a gentle simmer, then reduce the heat and cook uncovered for 20 to 25 minutes. You're looking for a sauce that coats a spoon — not thick like a jam, not thin like a jus, but somewhere between the two. Glossy and moving.

Taste it. This is the moment that matters. Elderberry carries natural tannin and bitterness that needs to be in conversation with the acid and the sweet, not drowning either of them. If it's too sharp, a little more honey. If it's too sweet, a small splash more vinegar. If it feels flat, more salt. Season properly — this sauce is bold and it needs seasoning to match.

Pull out the thyme stalks. If you want a smooth sauce, pass it through a fine sieve and push firmly. If you like texture — which I usually do — leave it roughly as it is or use the back of a spoon to crush it down without straining.

## Field Maple Syrup & Hay-Smoked Roots

# Field Maple Syrup & Hay-Smoked Roots

This is a tree you walk past every day and never think to tap. Field maple runs thin and early, shy with its sugar compared to its Canadian cousins, but that restraint is part of what makes it worth the trouble. You get a syrup that doesn't bully the plate—something floral, almost honeyed, with a green note that sugar maple never touches. I pair it here with roots because both come from the same cold ground, and because the syrup wants something earthy to push against.

You'll need about fifteen litres of sap to get one litre of syrup. Start collecting when the nights are still frosty and the days open warm. The flow follows the weather.

**For the roots:**
- 2 large parsnips
- 2 medium carrots
- 1 celeriac, peeled
- 150g unsalted butter
- A handful of clean hay (untreated, from a trusted source)
- Sea salt
- Black pepper

**For the syrup:**
- 5 litres field maple sap
- 1 vanilla pod, split
- The zest of one unwaxed lemon

**To finish:**
- A few sprigs of wood sorrel or sheep's sorrel
- Flaky salt

First, get the syrup going. Pour the sap into your widest pan—the more surface area, the better. Drop in the vanilla and lemon zest. Bring it to a steady simmer and let it reduce. This will take hours. Do not walk away from it toward the end. When it starts to look like syrup—thick enough to coat a spoon, bubbling slow and heavy—pull it off. Strain it through a fine sieve while it's still warm. You should have about enough to fill a small jar. It keeps for months in the fridge, but you'll use it before then.

For the roots, light a fire in your grill or get your oven as hot as it will go—250°C if it'll take it. Cut the parsnips and carrots into fat batons, about the thickness of your thumb. Dice the celeriac into chunks that feel generous in the hand. Toss everything in the butter, salt, and pepper.

Lay the hay in a roasting tray, pile the roots on top, and cover tightly with foil. If you're on the grill, put the tray away from direct flame. Roast for forty minutes, then lift the foil and let them take colour for another fifteen. The hay gives them something you can't fake—a sweetness that tastes of barns and late autumn.

To serve, pile the roots on a warm plate, spoon the field maple syrup over them while it's still loose, and tear the sorrel over the top. Finish with flaky salt.

**A variation:** This syrup is good on soft cheese—goat's curd or a washed rind. Pour it warm, eat it with dark bread and the first pickled walnuts of the season.

## Fireweed Shoots with Lemon, Pine, and Soft Cheese

# Fireweed Shoots with Lemon, Pine, and Soft Cheese

There’s a brief window in early spring—maybe three weeks, maybe less—when fireweed shoots push through the soil and taste like something worth getting your hands dirty for. Before the stem fibres toughen and that astringent, soapy character takes hold, the young shoots are tender, faintly sweet, with a greenness that carries the memory of ash and regrowth. They deserve something simple that doesn’t argue with them.

This dish treats the shoot like what it is: a seasonal moment. You can scale it from a single plate to a tray for eight, and it works as a starter, a light lunch, or alongside grilled fish or lamb.

**Ingredients**  
For two as a starter:  
- 200g fireweed shoots, harvested when no taller than your hand, tips still curled  
- 1 lemon  
- A small handful of young pine needles or a few sprigs of pine-infused fir  
- 100g soft goat’s cheese or a young sheep’s milk curd  
- Good olive oil  
- Flaky salt, black pepper  
- Optional: a few fireweed flowers (in season) or early blossoms from the same patch

**Method**

Pick your shoots when the ground is still cold and the days are stretching. Cut them close to the soil line, discarding any that feel woody or have begun to leaf out fully. Rinse them well—fireweed colonises disturbed ground, and ash or grit clings to the base. Pat dry.

Bring a shallow pan of salted water to a rolling boil. Drop the shoots in for ninety seconds—no longer. The colour will deepen from pale green to a vivid, almost luminous jade. Drain immediately and spread on a cloth. Don’t shock them in ice water; you want them to carry residual warmth.

While the shoots drain, zest half the lemon into a small bowl. Add a generous pinch of salt, a grind of pepper, and three tablespoons of olive oil. Whisk. Taste it. It should be bright and sharp, with the oil carrying it rather than drowning it.

Lay the warm shoots on a plate or a wooden board. Crumble the cheese over them in irregular pieces—not too fine, you want texture. Spoon the lemon-oil dressing over the top. Scatter the pine needles or fir tips like you mean it, not for show but because the resinous bite cuts through the cheese and lifts the vegetal note of the shoots.

Finish with a last turn of pepper and a few drops of olive oil from height. If you have flowers, tuck them in now. Serve immediately, with good bread to catch the dressing and the crumbles of cheese that fall to the plate.

**A variation**

If you have access to young fireweed roots—and you’re willing to dig—try this: scrub the roots clean, slice them thinly, and boil them in two changes of water until tender. Mash them with butter and a little salt. It’s not a revelation, but it is a quiet, honest root mash that anchors the shoots and cheese without competing. Serve them side by side, and let the contrast speak for itself.

They want fat, they want acidity, and they respond beautifully to heat that's direct and honest.

---

**Ingredients (serves 4 as a starter)**

- 300g goatsbeard budlings and young shoots, harvested before the leaves fully open
- 80g unsalted butter
- 2 tbsp wild garlic capers (or good-quality salted capers, rinsed)

**Method**

## Grand Fir Shoot Tea with Honey, Lemon, and Wild Ferment Shrub

# Grand Fir Shoot Tea with Honey, Lemon, and Wild Ferment Shrub

**Abies grandis — Grand Fir**

---

The young spring tips of grand fir carry something you don't forget — a clean, resinous brightness that sits somewhere between citrus zest, pine, and cold mountain air. It's not aggressive the way some conifers can be; it's almost generous, open, almost sweet. That quality makes it worth building around rather than using as a footnote.

---

**Serves 4**

**For the fir shoot tea base:**
- 20–25 young grand fir shoot tips (bright green, just unfurling — spring harvest only)
- 750ml fresh cold water
- 1 tbsp raw local honey, plus more to taste
- Half a lemon, juice only
- Small pinch of flaky sea salt

**For the wild shrub (make 3–5 days ahead):**
- 150ml raw apple cider vinegar (with the mother)
- 150g crab apples or sharp eating apples, roughly chopped, skin on
- 80g raw cane sugar or honey
- 4–5 additional grand fir shoot tips
- A thumb of fresh ginger, sliced thin

**To finish:**
- Sparkling water or cold still water
- A few reserved raw fir tips and a curl of lemon peel per glass

---

**Method**

Start with the shrub, because it needs time. Combine the vinegar, sugar, chopped apple, ginger, and the handful of fir tips in a clean jar. Stir until the sugar begins to dissolve, seal it, and leave it somewhere cool and dark for three to five days. Shake it once a day. What you're doing here is letting the resinous oils in the fir marry with the acidity of the vinegar and the pectin from the apple skin — it becomes something genuinely complex, almost medicinal in the best sense. Strain it through a fine sieve, pressing the solids. Bottle it. It will keep in the fridge for several weeks.

For the tea, bring your cold water up slowly — don't rush it to a boil. You want it to reach around 85–90°C, the kind of temperature where steam is rising steadily but there's no aggressive rolling. Drop your fir tips in. They'll immediately begin to release their oils into the water, and the smell alone will tell you this is working. Let them steep for eight to ten minutes. The water will shift to a pale straw colour with a faint green tint. Strain it, then while it's still warm, stir in the honey and lemon juice. Taste it. It should be clean and aromatic — the honey softening the resin, the lemon lifting it. Add your pinch of salt. That's not a mistake; it opens the whole thing up.

To serve cold, let the tea cool fully, then pour over ice. Add a generous splash — a tablespoon or two — of the fir shrub per glass. Top with sparkling water if you want movement, still if you want the flavour to sit and breathe. Garnish with one or two raw shoot tips and a curl of lemon peel. The raw tips on top are not decoration. They carry live oils that continue to perfume the drink as you bring it to your mouth. That matters.

Served warm, skip the shrub and simply adjust honey and lemon, perhaps adding a very thin slice of fresh ginger to each cup before you pour.

---

**Variation:** Replace the apple cider vinegar in the shrub with a live kombucha base for something softer and more effervescent. If you can find Douglas fir tips growing nearby, blend a few in with the grand fir — they're sharper and more citrus-forward, and the two together have a real depth that either one alone doesn't quite reach.

Great yellow-cress is a plant that wants to be noticed. It's got real backbone — that hot, peppery bite of its cousins watercress and arugula, but somehow wilder, more mineral. ## Ground Elder Soup with Toasted Coriander, Lemon, and Crème Fraîche

# Ground Elder Soup with Toasted Coriander, Lemon, and Crème Fraîche

---

Ground elder is a plant that divides people sharply, and that division is itself a clue to how to cook it. There's a wild, mineral tang to it — somewhere between flat-leaf parsley and celery leaf, with a slight bitterness underneath that softens beautifully when heat is applied. You want it young, gathered before the flowers come, when that vitality is concentrated in the leaf rather than dispersed into the bloom.

---

**Ingredients — serves 4**

500g young ground elder leaves, washed, coarser stems removed
1 large onion, diced
3 cloves garlic, sliced
2 sticks celery, sliced
1 medium floury potato, peeled and diced
1 litre good vegetable stock
Zest and juice of 1 lemon
1 tsp whole coriander seeds
2 tbsp olive oil
Sea salt and black pepper
4 tbsp crème fraîche, to finish
A few raw young ground elder leaves, very finely sliced, to serve

---

**Method**

Start with the coriander seeds in a dry pan over a medium heat. Let them toast until they shift colour slightly and the kitchen starts to smell like somewhere warmer than England. Crack them loosely in a pestle and mortar — you want texture, not powder — and set aside.

Warm the olive oil in a heavy-bottomed pot, then add the onion and celery with a good pinch of salt. Cook gently for eight to ten minutes. You're building a base here, not browning anything — this is about sweetness and depth, not colour. Add the garlic and potato, give it another couple of minutes, then pour in the stock and bring it to a gentle simmer. Let the potato cook fully — around fifteen minutes — so it will give the soup body without any starchiness catching you.

Now add the ground elder. All of it. It will look alarming, like too much, but it will drop into the heat and halve in seconds. Stir it through, let it wilt completely, then pull the pot off the heat almost immediately. You want to keep the colour and preserve the vitality of the leaf — the longer you cook it after this point, the more of that bright, wild character you lose.

Blend until completely smooth. Taste it. Add the lemon juice gradually — you want acidity to lift the mineral edge without flattening it, so go carefully and trust your palate. Season with salt and pepper. If it feels slightly too robust, a small splash of water or extra stock will bring it into balance.

Ladle into warm bowls. Put a generous spoonful of crème fraîche in the centre — the fat and the slight sourness are exactly what this soup needs against its green intensity. Scatter over the cracked coriander, a few strands of raw young ground elder, and finish with the lemon zest.

---

**Variation**

If you're cooking this earlier in spring when wild garlic is still up, add a handful of the leaves alongside the ground elder at the end. They are natural companions in the hedgerow and on the plate — the allium rounds off the sharper edges of the ground elder and gives the whole thing a rounder, more complete flavour. A piece of good sourdough, grilled hard with olive oil, is the only accompaniment you need.

## Hawthorn Leaf Tea with Roasted Seed Broth and Charred Shoots

# Hawthorn Leaf Tea with Roasted Seed Broth and Charred Shoots

Hawthorn leaves are often overlooked in favour of the berries, but they carry something sharper, more alive — a subtle astringency that sits between green tea and orchard fruit. The shoots have a real nutty sweetness that works beautifully against char. Together, they make a dish that feels like spring captured in a bowl: warming, mineral, and genuinely restorative without sentimentality.

**Serves 2–3**

Fresh hawthorn leaves (young, from the tips), a small handful  
Hawthorn shoots, about 8–10  
Hawthorn berries (dried or fresh), 3 tablespoons  
Water, 500ml  
Sea salt  
A knob of good butter  
A pinch of smoked sea salt (optional, for depth)

**Method**

Start with the broth, which is where the real work happens. Toast your hawthorn berries in a dry pan over medium heat for about 3 minutes — this wakes them up, draws out their earthiness. Crack them gently with the back of a spoon; you want to expose those seeds without pulverising them into dust. Pour 500ml cold water over them and bring to a simmer. Let it go for 15 minutes. The liquid should deepen slightly and take on a subtle tannin edge, almost like weak red wine. Taste it. It should taste of stone and autumn. Strain it through muslin or a fine sieve.

While that's happening, get your shoots ready. Pick the tender top 5–6cm of each shoot, discarding the woody base. Blanch them in salted boiling water for about 4 minutes until they're tender but still have a slight resistance. Drain them well and lay them out on a cloth.

Heat a cast iron pan or griddle until it's properly hot. A touch of butter or a light oil. Lay the shoots flat and let them char — about 90 seconds each side, no fussing. They should have dark caramelised patches and smell nutty and alive. Season with sea salt the moment they come off the heat.

Now for the tea: brew your fresh hawthorn leaves in a separate pot with about 200ml of just-boiled water. Let them steep for 4–5 minutes — any longer and they turn astringent in a way that bites. Strain gently.

To serve: pour the warmed hawthorn seed broth into shallow bowls, then carefully add a small pour of the leaf tea so the colours don't entirely marry (there's something nice about the visual separation). Drizzle the coriander oil deliberately around it and across the surface. Finish with two or three whole lemon balm leaves placed with intention, not scattered. The leaves will bruise slightly from the cold and that's fine — that bruising releases more of what you want.

---

**Variation:** If you're cooking on fire or want a warmer direction entirely, infuse the lemon balm leaves into warm butter with a little honey and use it to finish a whole roasted sea bream. The herb carries the same principles — brightness, vitality, that medicinal-citrus note — but heat transforms it differently, quieter and more resinous. Worth knowing.

## Nipplewort with Anchovy, Garlic, and Sourdough Breadcrumbs

# Nipplewort with Anchovy, Garlic, and Sourdough Breadcrumbs

Nipplewort arrives in spring with a particular kind of bitterness — not aggressive, more like a clean radish bite with green backbone. It's tender before flowering, holds its shape better than spinach when cooked, and has enough character to stand alongside assertive flavours without disappearing. This is a dish that works as a side or a light main, and it's the kind of thing you make when the plant is abundant and you want to cook without fuss.

**Ingredients**

800g nipplewort leaves and young shoots, washed  
4 anchovy fillets  
4 cloves garlic, thinly sliced  
100g sourdough breadcrumbs (day-old bread, torn and dried in a low oven)  
60ml good olive oil  
Fleur de sel and black pepper  
Lemon, cut into wedges  
A knob of butter

**Method**

Warm the olive oil in a wide, heavy pan over medium heat. Add the sliced garlic and let it move around gently until it softens and turns golden — this takes about three minutes. Don't rush it. Add the anchovy fillets and stir them into the oil. They'll break apart and dissolve, giving you a base that's salty, umami-rich, and deeply savoury.

Increase the heat slightly and add all the nipplewort at once. It will look impossible — a great mound of green — but it collapses in seconds. Stir constantly for two or three minutes until the leaves are completely wilted and any excess moisture is driven off. This matters: you want them tender but not soupy. Taste as you go. Season with a small pinch of fleur de sel (the anchovy is already salty) and a few turns of black pepper.

While the greens finish, toast the breadcrumbs in a dry pan over medium-high heat, moving them constantly until they're golden and smell of toasted grain. The bitterness of the nipplewort plays directly against the salt and umami of the anchovy, the breadcrumbs add textural contrast and nuttiness, and the lemon brings everything into focus. It's generous, simple, and tastes exactly of what it is.

If you have access to wild garlic leaves or ramson in the same season, swap half the garlic cloves for a handful of the leaves added just before serving — the sweetness softens the bitter edge and feels seasonal.

## Ox-Eye Daisy Shoots with Charred Spring Onions, Labneh, and Preserved Lemon

# Ox-Eye Daisy Shoots with Charred Spring Onions, Labneh, and Preserved Lemon

---

Ox-eye daisy is one of those plants that rewards boldness — the young spring shoots carry a genuine bitterness and a faint herbal pungency that lands somewhere between wild rocket and chrysanthemum leaf. That edge is the point. This recipe leans into it rather than apologising for it, pairing the shoots with the fat acidity of labneh and the salt-brightness of preserved lemon to create something that feels like early spring on a plate.

---

**Serves 4 as a starter or generous side**

**For the shoots and dressing**
- 80g young ox-eye daisy shoots, picked before flowering, washed and dried
- 1 bunch spring onions (about 8)
- 3 tbsp good olive oil
- 1 preserved lemon quarter, flesh scraped away, skin finely sliced
- 1 tsp raw honey
- 1 tbsp lemon juice, freshly squeezed
- Flaked sea salt

**For the base**
- 250g labneh (or thick strained yoghurt left overnight in muslin)
- 1 small garlic clove, grated to a paste
- 2 tbsp olive oil

**To finish**
- A small handful of ox-eye daisy flowers if they are just beginning to open — petals only
- Toasted nigella seeds, a pinch
- A few young dandelion leaves if you have them

---

**Method**

Start with the labneh. Work the grated garlic and olive oil through it with a spoon until it is smooth and just loose enough to spread. Taste it — it should be tangy, garlicky, and rich without being heavy. Set it aside at room temperature. Cold labneh on a plate is a missed opportunity.

Get a dry cast iron pan or griddle genuinely hot. Trim the spring onions, dress them lightly with olive oil and a pinch of salt, then lay them down and leave them. Resist moving them. You want proper blackening on the outside, softness all the way through — three to four minutes a side depending on thickness. When they come off, let them rest for a moment, then slice them on a diagonal into pieces roughly three centimetres long, keeping all the charred edges intact.

While the onions are resting, make the dressing. Whisk the honey and lemon juice together first, then add the olive oil slowly and whisk until it emulsifies loosely. Fold in the preserved lemon skin. This should taste sharp, sweet, and a little briny — check it, and adjust.

Now the daisy shoots. These are pungent, so use your judgement here. If they are very young and tender, you can be generous. If they are more developed, use them more sparingly and let the other greens carry more weight. Toss the shoots with the dandelion leaves if you are using them, dress them lightly — just enough to coat — and taste before you do anything else.

To plate, spread the labneh across the base of a wide shallow bowl or plate, using the back of a spoon to create some movement in it rather than smoothing it flat. Lay the charred spring onions across the labneh, then pile the dressed greens on top. Finish with the nigella seeds and scatter the daisy petals last, just before it goes to the table.

---

**A note on variation:** This works beautifully alongside spiced lamb cutlets or a whole roasted sea bream. If you want to push the Levantine character further, a few drops of pomegranate molasses in the dressing in place of honey will deepen the whole thing considerably.

## Pellitory-of-the-Wall with Whipped Sheep's Curd, Preserved Lemon & Toasted Dukkah

# Pellitory-of-the-Wall with Whipped Sheep's Curd, Preserved Lemon & Toasted Dukkah

---

Parietaria judaica grows out of Sheffield's stone walls like it owns them — and in a sense, it does. It belongs to the nettle family, which tells you something immediately: there's mineral depth here, a quiet iron-green vitality that you don't get from spinach or even good watercress. The young shoots are tender, slightly mucilaginous when warm, and carry a clean, faintly herbaceous flavour that sits somewhere between young nettle and mâche. It's a wall plant, a city plant, ancient and abundant — and it deserves a plate that honours that.

---

**Serves 4 as a starter**

For the base:
- 250g fresh sheep's curd (or a good soft goat's curd if that's what you have)
- 2 tbsp good olive oil
- Zest of half a lemon
- Sea salt

For the pellitory:
- A generous double handful of young Parietaria judaica shoots — picked before flowering, top 5–8cm, washed carefully
- 1 small clove of garlic, very thinly sliced
- 1 tbsp olive oil
- Squeeze of lemon juice

To finish:
- 2 tbsp dukkah, toasted dry in a pan until just fragrant
- Half a preserved lemon, pith removed, skin finely sliced into thin strips
- A few drops of good olive oil to dress
- Flaky salt

---

**Method**

Start with the curd. Put it in a bowl, add the olive oil and lemon zest, season generously with sea salt, and beat it until it's loose and voluptuous — not quite pourable, but with a softness that will spread easily. You want them softened but still holding their green — vivid, not collapsed. Season with a small pinch of salt and a squeeze of lemon, then take the pan off the heat immediately.

To plate: spoon the whipped curd onto each plate in a generous, unhurried swoosh. Lay the warm pellitory on top — pile it with some intention, let it sit with a little height. Scatter the preserved lemon strips across, then the dukkah. Finish with a thread of good olive oil and a few flakes of salt.

---

**A note on variation:** If you're eating this in late spring when wild garlic is still around, a few torn wild garlic leaves folded through the pellitory as it comes off the heat will deepen the whole thing considerably. Alternatively, a soft-boiled egg — yolk still running — placed alongside turns this into a meal proper.

## Plantain Leaf Broth with Charred Alliums and Toasted Seeds

# Plantain Leaf Broth with Charred Alliums and Toasted Seeds

Broadleaf plantain is everywhere—growing through cracks in Sheffield pavements, in grass verges, on disturbed ground. The leaves carry a subtle bitterness that sits somewhere between sorrel and dock, with an almost mineral quality underneath. That fibrous structure people complain about? It's exactly what makes this work as a broth. When you cook it properly, those strands break down and give the liquid body and a gentle grip on the palate. The seeds are worth the effort too—nutty, dense, full of B vitamins. This is a dish about extracting maximum character from a plant most people walk past.

**Serves 4 as a light main or 6 as a first course**

A good bunch of young plantain leaves (roughly 300g before cleaning)  
2 large leeks, white and pale green parts only, halved lengthwise  
1 bunch spring onions or small red onions  
2 tablespoons plantain seeds, toasted  
A knob of good butter  
1 litre light stock (vegetable or chicken, nothing heavy)  
Sea salt and cracked black pepper  
A squeeze of lemon  
Extra virgin olive oil  

**Method**

Pick the leaves from plantain plants that are growing in clean ground—away from roads. Pinch out the youngest leaves from the center of each rosette. Rinse them very well. Hold each leaf by the tip and strip it against the grain with your thumb to remove the worst of the fibrous strands. You're not trying to make them tender—you're breaking the structure enough that they'll release flavor into the broth. Rough chop what you've got.

Char your leeks hard in a dry pan or over flame until the outsides blacken and soften. The bitterness this creates is essential—it anchors everything. Do the same with your spring onions or small onions. Set them aside.

Bring your stock to a simmer. Add the plantain leaves and let them cook for eight or nine minutes. They'll discolor the broth slightly—don't worry about this, it's natural. The bitterness will mellow as the leaves release their tannins. Taste as you go. When the broth tastes clean and slightly green and the leaves have surrendered, strain the whole thing through a fine sieve, pressing gently on the leaves to extract their essence. Discard what's left in the sieve.

Pour the broth back into the pan. Add your charred leeks and onions. Warm everything through. Toast your plantain seeds in a dry pan until they smell nutty and begin to pop slightly. Grind them roughly with a pinch of sea salt.

Finish the broth with a knob of butter whisked in, a squeeze of lemon, and a good turn of black pepper. Ladle into bowls. Scatter the toasted seeds over the top. Drizzle with olive oil.

**Variation**: In winter, when plantain leaves are tougher and more fibrous, dry them instead and make a proper tea—steep them for five minutes in hot water with a slice of ginger and a touch of honey. The bitterness becomes medicinal and grounding. It's what the plant wants to be at that point in the year.

# Poppy Seed & Roasted Garlic Flatbread with Whipped Feta and Preserved Lemon

Papaver somniferum seeds are one of the most underused things in a serious kitchen. 300g strong white flour

Don't fight the dough — let it rest for a minute if it springs back, then come back to it. Scatter the extra poppy seeds onto your work surface and press each flatbread down into them so the seeds embed into the underside. Then press a few more onto the top surface.

## Prickly Lettuce with Preserved Lemon, Toasted Cumin Oil & Soft-Boiled Egg

# Prickly Lettuce with Preserved Lemon, Toasted Cumin Oil & Soft-Boiled Egg

---

Lactuca serriola is the wild ancestor of the lettuce you grew up with, and it carries that lineage with a sharpness cultivated varieties have had carefully bred out of them. Caught young — before the stem bolts and the latex turns aggressive — the leaves have a clean, slightly milky bitterness that sits beautifully against fat, acid, and warmth. Think of it less like salad and more like a green with something to say.

---

**Serves 2**

8–10 young Lactuca serriola leaves, picked before flowering, central spine removed if coarse

2 eggs, room temperature

1 preserved lemon quarter, flesh scraped, skin finely sliced

1 tsp cumin seeds

4 tbsp good olive oil

1 small garlic clove

A few sprigs of fresh coriander or flat parsley

Flaked salt

A squeeze of fresh lemon

---

**Method**

Start with the leaves. If you've picked them yourself, you'll know what you're working with — young plants from a hedgerow or waste ground, the leaves still relatively flat and undefended, with that faint milky smell when torn. Wash them well, shake them dry, and taste one raw. What you're feeling for is the level of bitterness: mild and pleasant, or edging toward sharp? That tells you whether you're eating these raw, wilting them briefly, or blanching quickly in salted water for thirty seconds before pressing dry. Most early-season leaves can go straight to the plate. Anything harvested once the plant is starting to stretch and think about flowering will need that quick blanch to take the edge off.

Bring a small pot of water to a gentle boil. Lower your eggs in and cook them for six and a half minutes. Into iced water immediately — you want a yolk that's just-set, still golden and slightly molten at the centre.

While the eggs cool, make your cumin oil. Put a small pan on a medium heat, add the cumin seeds dry, and let them move around until you can smell them — forty seconds, maybe a minute. Don't walk away. Add the olive oil and drop the heat right down. Slice the garlic thinly and let it drift in the warm oil for two minutes. It shouldn't colour. You're infusing, not frying. Take it off the heat, add the preserved lemon strips, and leave everything to meld while you build the plate.

Lay the leaves out — some raw and open, some folded, a little architecture without fussiness. Peel the eggs and halve them, settle them into the leaves. Spoon the cumin oil generously over everything, making sure some of the lemon strips and whole seeds find their way across the plate. Tear the herbs over the top. A small pinch of flaked salt on the egg yolk. A squeeze of lemon to lift it all.

Eat straight away while the oil is still warm against the cool leaves.

---

**Variation:** If you have access to a fire or a griddle, blister the older leaves directly over heat for ten seconds each side before dressing. That char turns the bitterness into something smoky and complex — closer to wilted bitter radicchio territory — and it pairs well with a spoonful of labneh underneath and a scattering of pomegranate seeds.

## Ramsons & New Potato Soup with Wild Garlic Oil and Fermented Bulbil Capers

# Ramsons & New Potato Soup with Wild Garlic Oil and Fermented Bulbil Capers

Ramsons arrive in the woods just when you need them most — that late winter, early spring moment when everything still feels grey and the body is quietly craving something green and alive. The leaves carry a garlic flavour that's genuinely milder than the bulb, softer than you expect, and when you cook them briefly into something warm, they give up a sweetness that the raw leaf only hints at. This soup is built around that sweetness, anchored by waxy new potatoes and finished with a raw ramsons oil that keeps one foot firmly in the wild.

---

**Serves 4**

*For the soup*
500g new potatoes, scrubbed and halved
150g ramsons leaves, washed and roughly torn — keep a small handful back for the oil
1 medium white onion, thinly sliced
2 sticks celery, sliced
1 litre good vegetable stock, warm
3 tbsp olive oil
Sea salt and white pepper

*For the ramsons oil*
Large handful of ramsons leaves (the ones you held back)
100ml light extra virgin olive oil
Pinch of fine sea salt

*To finish*
2 tbsp fermented ramsons bulbils (see note below), or good quality capers if you haven't made your own yet
A little crème fraîche or full-fat yoghurt — optional, but generous
Ramsons flowers if they're available — a few scattered over the top

---

**Method**

Start with a wide, heavy pot over a medium flame. Pour in the olive oil and let it warm before you add the onion and celery. You're not rushing this — cook them down slowly for eight to ten minutes until they're properly soft and beginning to smell sweet. Season with salt here, early, and let the vegetables carry that seasoning all the way through.

Add the potatoes and stir everything together so the oil coats them. Pour in the warm stock — warm matters, it keeps the cooking momentum going — then bring the whole thing to a gentle simmer. Cover partially and let it cook for around fifteen minutes, until the potatoes are completely tender and beginning to give at the edges.

Now add the ramsons leaves. All of them, directly into the pot. Stir them down and cook for no more than two minutes. You want them wilted and integrated, not boiled into something dull. Pull the pot off the heat immediately and blend the soup smooth — a jug blender gives you a better result than a stick blender here, smoother and more vivid in colour. Taste carefully. Adjust the salt. Add white pepper. The soup should be a deep, confident green, and the flavour should land somewhere between leek and garlic without quite being either.

For the ramsons oil, blend the raw leaves with the olive oil and a pinch of salt until completely smooth and brilliant green. If you have a high-speed blender, thirty seconds is enough. Strain it through a fine sieve or muslin if you want it clean and glossy, or leave it as it is for something wilder. Either way, it keeps in a small jar in the fridge for three or four days, and the colour stays extraordinary.

Serve the soup in warm bowls — this matters more than people give it credit for. A spoonful of crème fraîche or thick yoghurt dropped in the centre, a drizzle of the ramsons oil around it, and four or five fermented bulbils placed with intention rather than scattered carelessly. If you've got the flowers, lay them on last. They're stronger than the leaves and they'll make their presence known.

## Raspberry Leaf Tea with Fermented Raspberries and Burnt Honey

# Raspberry Leaf Tea with Fermented Raspberries and Burnt Honey

Raspberry leaf carries a particular earthiness — mineral, slightly astringent, with a green tea quality that deepens when dried properly. The fruit itself is fragile and fleeting, but when you ferment it gently, it becomes something more stable, more profound. This isn't a dessert. It's a palate-cleanser, a digestif, something you drink when you want to taste the season long after it's passed.

**For the tea (serves 4)**

Dried raspberry leaves — a large handful, ideally harvested in early summer and dried in shade until papery
Water — 600ml, just off the boil
Sea salt — a small pinch

**For the fermented raspberries**

Fresh raspberries — 300g, ripe but not collapsing
Caster sugar — 40g
Whey or a tablespoon of good live yoghurt (or just leave it to wild ferment)

**For the burnt honey finish**

Honey — 3 tablespoons, ideally something local and unheated
Butter — a small knob

---

**Method**

Start with the ferment three or four days before you want to serve this. Put the raspberries in a clean jar with the sugar and your culture — whey works beautifully, or if you're confident in your kitchen's wild yeasts, just sugar them and leave the lid loose. You're looking for gentle bubbling, a deepening colour, a smell that's still fruity but with an edge of fermentation to it. Taste as you go. Stop it when it feels right — somewhere between fresh and properly sour. This is intuition work.

---

## Red Clover Flower Tea Cake with Dried Leaf Crumb and Honey Glaze

# Red Clover Flower Tea Cake with Dried Leaf Crumb and Honey Glaze

Red clover carries more than most people give it credit for. The flowers have a genuine sweetness — faintly honeyed, slightly grassy, with a warm floral depth that intensifies when dried. The dried leaves bring something unexpected: a vanilla-like quality that earns its place in baking the same way tonka or good vanilla paste does, quietly anchoring everything around it.

---

**Ingredients**

*For the cake*
- 200g unsalted butter, softened
- 180g unrefined golden caster sugar
- 3 eggs
- 220g plain flour
- 1½ tsp baking powder
- Pinch of fine sea salt
- 80ml whole milk
- 2 tbsp dried red clover flowers, loosely packed, plus extra to finish
- 1 tbsp dried red clover leaves, ground to a fine powder in a spice grinder

*For the honey glaze*
- 3 tbsp good wildflower or heather honey
- 1 tbsp warm water
- A few drops of lemon juice

*For the dried leaf crumb*
- 25g soft breadcrumbs, toasted golden
- 1 tsp dried red clover leaf powder
- Pinch of flaked sea salt

---

**Method**

Start the night before if you can. Warm the milk gently — not quite to a simmer — and steep the dried clover flowers in it for at least an hour, or overnight covered in the fridge. What you're drawing out is that honeyed, faintly meadow sweetness. When you're ready to bake, strain the milk and press the flowers to get everything out of them. Discard the spent flowers or scatter them in the garden.

Heat the oven to 170°C fan. Butter and lightly flour a 900g loaf tin or a 20cm round cake tin — your call.

Cream the butter and sugar together properly. Take your time here. You want it pale and genuinely aerated — five minutes at least with a stand mixer, or patient work with a wooden spoon and some will. Beat in the eggs one at a time, adding a spoonful of flour with the second and third to keep it from curdling.

Sift together the flour, baking powder, salt, and clover leaf powder. The powder will make the mixture look faintly dusty green-grey — don't worry, that's honest. Fold the dry ingredients into the butter mixture in three additions, alternating with the clover-infused milk, beginning and ending with flour. The batter should be smooth, loose enough to fall from a spoon with a little encouragement.

Pour into your prepared tin and bake for 40–45 minutes until a skewer comes out clean and the top has a deep golden colour with slight spring underfoot. Let it rest in the tin for ten minutes, then turn it out onto a wire rack.

While it's still warm — not hot, just warm — make the glaze by stirring the honey, water, and lemon together until runny. Brush it over the top and sides generously. You want it to sink in and leave a slight sheen, not sit on the surface.

Mix the toasted breadcrumbs with the remaining clover leaf powder and the flaked salt in a small bowl. Scatter this across the glazed top while the glaze is still tacky so it clings. Finish with a small handful of whole dried clover flowers pressed lightly into the crumb — they'll catch the light and look genuinely beautiful without trying.

Slice it when it's fully cool, though I rarely manage that.

---

**To push it further:** Make a simple cream cheese or labneh to serve alongside — softened with a little honey and a few drops of rosewater, because red clover and rose sit in the same meadow and they know each other well.

## Red Spruce Shoot Oil, Wild Garlic Butter & New Potato Salad with Pickled Spruce Tips

# Red Spruce Shoot Oil, Wild Garlic Butter & New Potato Salad with Pickled Spruce Tips

---

Red spruce tips carry something genuinely alive — a bright, resinous citrus that sits between pine and lemon verbena with a mineral edge underneath. They arrive in late spring when the new growth is still tender and pale green, and that's your window. This is a dish built around that vitality: a warm potato salad that lets the spruce do what it actually does, which is cut through richness and lift everything around it.

---

**Ingredients** *(serves 4)*

For the pickled spruce tips:
- 60g young red spruce shoot tips, gathered when pale and just unfurling
- 150ml white wine vinegar
- 150ml water
- 1 tbsp honey
- ½ tsp flaked sea salt

For the spruce oil:
- 30g red spruce tips
- 150ml cold-pressed rapeseed oil

For the wild garlic butter:
- 150g good unsalted butter, softened
- 25g wild garlic leaves, very finely chopped
- Zest of half a lemon
- Pinch of flaked salt

For the salad:
- 700g waxy new potatoes — Charlotte or Pink Fir if you can get them
- 4 soft-boiled eggs
- A handful of pea shoots or wood sorrel if you're near it
- Flaked sea salt and black pepper

---

**Method**

Start the pickled spruce tips a day ahead if you can, though a few hours will do the work. Bring the vinegar, water, honey and salt to a gentle simmer until the honey dissolves, then pour it hot over the spruce tips in a sterilised jar. They'll soften slightly and turn a deeper, more olive green — that's fine. What you're after is that bright resinous note held in acid, which becomes something like pickled citrus rind with a forest floor underneath. Set them aside.

For the oil, blanch the spruce tips in boiling salted water for thirty seconds, then straight into ice water. Squeeze out the excess moisture, put them in a blender with the rapeseed oil and blitz hard for two minutes — the oil will turn a vivid, living green. Pass it through a fine sieve or muslin and you'll have something that smells like standing in a spruce forest on a cold morning. Keep it in a small jar in the fridge and use it within four days while it still has that vitality.

Make the wild garlic butter while you're thinking about it — just beat the chopped wild garlic, lemon zest and salt into the softened butter until it's unified and fragrant. Roll it in greaseproof paper if you want to be organised about it.

## Roasted Apple with Brown Butter, Preserved Lemon & Wild Thyme

# Roasted Apple with Brown Butter, Preserved Lemon & Wild Thyme

The apple is one of those fruits that people underestimate precisely because they know it so well. Given heat and fat, something shifts — the sugars concentrate, the flesh softens into something almost custardy at its core while the skin catches and caramelises at the edges. This recipe asks you to treat the apple with the same seriousness you'd bring to any stone fruit at its peak.

---

**Serves 4**

4 firm, flavourful apples — Cox, Blenheim Orange, or a good heritage variety if you can find them. Avoid anything mealy.
80g unsalted butter
1 preserved lemon, flesh discarded, skin finely sliced
2 tablespoons runny honey
A generous handful of fresh wild thyme — garden thyme works, but wild carries more intensity
1 teaspoon ground cinnamon
Sea salt — just a pinch, and don't skip it
50ml dry cider or white wine
Crème fraîche or labneh to serve

---

**Method**

Start with your oven at 190°C. Core the apples but leave them whole — you want them to hold their shape and cook from the inside out. Score the skin around the equator of each apple with a sharp knife, just lightly, so they don't burst. This is an old technique and it matters.

4 medium courgettes, halved lengthways
Good olive oil
Sea salt and black pepper

---

- 300g good goat's curd, or a soft, fresh goat's cheese if curd isn't available
- 2 tablespoons crème fraîche
- 1 unwaxed lemon
- 1 tablespoon good wildflower honey, plus a little more to finish

- 30g unsalted butter
- A small bunch of fresh chives, finely cut

This is not a novelty bread. The inner bark of *Abies alba* carries a quiet, woody sweetness with something almost like toasted oats underneath—a soft, resinous warmth that asks for slow cooking, not flour blending into anonymity. Most people who dry and powder it are missing the point. Fresh inner bark, gently cooked, brings texture and a flavour that speaks of clean mountain air. This porridge treats it with the respect it deserves.

---

**For the fir porridge:**
- Inner bark of one mature *Abies alba* branch (approximately 80–100g fresh)
- 200g whole rye grains, soaked overnight
- 1 litre good quality water or light vegetable stock
- 40g unsalted butter
- Sea salt

**For the fermented fir tip vinegar:**
- Fresh *Abies alba* tips (the bright green new growth, about 20g)
- 250ml raw apple cider vinegar (unpasteurised, with mother)
- 1 teaspoon raw honey

**To finish:**
- A handful of fresh fir tips (very young, pale green)
- Good quality olive oil
- Flaky sea salt

---

## Thyme, Lemon, and Honeyed Aubergine Stack with a Wild Garlic and Seta Crumb

# Thyme, Lemon, and Honeyed Aubergine Stack with a Wild Garlic and Seta Crumb

Thyme is one of the few herbs that can lead a dish without shouting. It’s got backbone. That quiet, almost medicinal warmth — camphor and earth and something like the inside of a sun-warmed stone — carries through slow cooking and deepens without turning bitter. I’ve cooked with it for nearly thirty years, and it still surprises me how well it holds its own against sweet things, dairy, and fungi. This recipe lets thyme sit at the centre. The aubergine soaks up lemon and honey; the yoghurt cools; the crumb brings wild garlic and a good seta mushroom for a bit of woodland depth. It’s a dish you can pull together on a Tuesday, but it looks like you meant it.

**Ingredients**  
*(for two, generously)*

- 2 medium aubergines
- 4 tablespoons good olive oil
- Juice of 1 lemon, plus a little extra for finishing
- 2 tablespoons runny honey — go for a clear, floral one if you can
- 4 or 5 generous sprigs of fresh thyme (Thymus vulgaris), leaves stripped and a few whole sprigs kept for garnish
- 2 cloves garlic, very finely sliced
- 200g thick Greek yoghurt
- A small handful of fresh wild garlic leaves (or a tablespoon of wild garlic pesto, if you're out of season)
- 100g seta mushrooms — or any firm, meaty mushroom, but seta has that umami you want here
- 50g fresh breadcrumbs (sourdough or a good country loaf)
- Salt and black pepper
- A pinch of dried chilli flakes (optional)

**Method**

Preheat your oven to 190°C. Slice the aubergines into rounds about two centimetres thick — not too thin, you want them to hold their shape. Salt them lightly and leave them in a colander for twenty minutes. This isn't about bitterness, it's about structure. Pat them dry.

Rub this all over the aubergine slices, then lay them on a baking tray in a single layer. Roast for twenty-five minutes, turning once, until they're golden and soft right through — they should almost collapse when you press them, but still hold their form.

While the aubergine roasts, get the crumb together. Finely chop the seta mushrooms — you want them the size of a peppercorn, not a paste. Heat a tablespoon of olive oil in a small pan and fry the mushroom pieces with a little salt until they start to colour and release their moisture. Take it off the heat and stir through the wild garlic — if you're using fresh leaves, chop them fine; if it's pesto, just fold it through. Set aside.

Season the yoghurt with a pinch of salt and a squeeze of lemon. Spread it over the base of your serving plate in a thick, uneven swoop. Stack the roasted aubergine slices on top — I like three per person, with a little of the roasting juices spooned between them. Pile the seta and wild garlic crumb over the top. Finish with a few whole thyme leaves, a twist of black pepper, and if you want a little heat, the dried chilli.

**Variation**

You can swap the seta for a firm, dried mushroom like porcini, rehydrated and chopped — the intensity will shift, but it still works. Or, if you want to move this into a main course, lay a piece of grilled mackerel or a fried egg alongside. The thyme carries both.

# Valerian Leaf Salt with Roasted Apple and Aged Goat's Curd

Valerian doesn't announce itself loudly — it earns your attention gradually. The leaves carry something resinous and slightly bitter, earthy in a way that reads almost mineral, and when you dry and grind them with good salt they become something genuinely structural: a finishing condiment with depth rather than heat, complexity rather than sharpness. The essential oil in both leaf and root is closely tied to apple flavour at a chemical level, which is not a coincidence — it's a direction.

---

**For the valerian leaf salt**
- 20g fresh young valerian leaves, harvested before flowering
- 40g flaked sea salt (Maldon or similar)
- Zest of half a lemon

**For the dish**
- 4 medium eating apples — Cox, Ribston Pippin, or a good heritage variety. Nothing too sweet, nothing too bland.

- 3 tablespoons good olive oil

- Small handful of fresh walnut halves
- A few young valerian leaves to finish, if you have them

---

**Start with the salt**, and give it time. Wash the valerian leaves and dry them thoroughly — you want no moisture left. Lay them on a rack and let them air-dry for a day, or put them in an oven set as low as it will go, door slightly ajar, for an hour or two. They should be crisp and dark, not burnt. Break the walnuts roughly over everything, add a drizzle of olive oil, and finish with a proper pinch of the valerian salt across the curd and the apple flesh. If you have a few small fresh valerian leaves, scatter them now — they're slightly bitter raw, which works well here as contrast. A little more lemon zest if the dish needs brightness.

---

# Water Mint Granita with Charred Cucumber and Labneh

## Mentha aquatica — Water Mint

Water mint is the wild ancestor behind most of what we recognise as mint flavour — but it carries something rawer and more resinous than cultivated varieties, a camphor edge that sits underneath the sweetness. That intensity means you use less and it gives more, which demands respect rather than restraint. This granita uses the cold to soften its pungency into something luminous, pairing it against the smoky bitterness of charred cucumber and the quiet acidity of labneh.

---

**For the granita**
500ml cold water
120g caster sugar
Large handful of fresh water mint leaves — roughly 25–30g, stripped from stems
Juice of 1 lemon

**For the charred cucumber**
2 medium cucumbers
Olive oil
Flaky sea salt
Small handful of water mint leaves, very finely chopped

**For the labneh**
500g full-fat Greek yoghurt, strained overnight through muslin
Good olive oil to finish
Pinch of flaky salt

**To serve**
A few small water mint leaves — the youngest ones you can find
Cold-pressed olive oil

---

## Method

Start the labneh the evening before. Spoon the yoghurt into a muslin cloth, tie it off, hang it over a bowl in the fridge, and leave it overnight. By morning you want something firm enough to hold a quenelle — silky, tangy, with enough body to anchor the plate.

For the granita, make a light syrup by bringing the water and sugar to a gentle simmer until the sugar dissolves completely, then pull it off the heat and let it cool for five minutes. You want it warm but not scalding before the mint goes in — if it's too hot you'll lose the top notes and be left with something medicinal. The warmth left in the cucumber will draw the mint out gently.

To build the plate, place a generous spoonful of labneh slightly off-centre, nudge it into a soft smear. Lay the warm charred cucumber alongside and over it. Spoon the granita last — directly before serving, from freezer to table — so it holds its structure long enough to matter. A few of the smallest, freshest water mint leaves over the top, a slow pour of cold-pressed olive oil across everything.

# Watercress, Burnt Butter & Hazelnut with a Soft Egg

# Wild Carrot Root with Burnt Honey, Hazelnut, and Charred Leaves

Wild carrot—the ancestor of every cultivated carrot you've ever eaten—carries something cultivated forms have lost: an aromatic intensity and a mineral edge that tastes like soil and memory. When you dig a young first-year root in autumn or early spring, tender and thin as a finger, you're holding something genuinely alive. The key is timing. Dig too late, dig a second-year plant, and you get woody bitterness. Pick at the right moment and you get sweetness with backbone—clean, white, faintly peppery, nothing like the bloated orange sugarbombs we're used to. This dish treats them simply: roasted until the skin just catches, dressed with the alchemy of burnt honey and the earthiness of hazelnut, finished with the carrot's own foraged leaves crisped and scattered.

80g hazelnuts, skins on
60g good honey (something with character—heather, wildflower)

Sea salt
Cold butter or good oil
Fleur de sel to finish

**Method**

Dig your carrots carefully—you want the whole taproot intact, and they should snap cleanly when bent, not flex. Wash them hard under cold water, scrubbing away soil with your thumbs. Leave the skin on. Trim the leaf crowns, but keep the leaves separate; they're part of the story.

Toast the hazelnuts in a dry pan over medium heat, turning often, until the skins loosen and the kernels smell warm and sweet. About 6 minutes. Tip them onto a cloth, rub them loose, then crush them roughly—you want pieces, not powder. Set aside.

Heat your oven to 200°C. Slice the carrots lengthwise, leaving the thinner ones whole. Toss them with a little oil, salt generously—more than feels right—and lay them cut-side down on a hot pan. Let them colour, don't move them around. This takes 4–5 minutes. You want real caramelization on the cut surface; that's where the flavour lives. Transfer to a roasting tray and finish in the oven for 12–15 minutes until they're tender when a knife slides through and the edges are slightly shrivelled.

While they roast, warm the honey in a small heavy pan over medium heat. Watch it carefully. As soon as it shifts from gold to amber—darker, almost smoking—pull it off the heat. Stir in a pinch of salt and a small knob of cold butter. The honey will seize and then smooth again. This should smell like caramel and deep sweetness, not burnt sugar.

Crisp the carrot leaves in a dry pan over high heat, tossing constantly, until they darken and become papery—2–3 minutes. They should smell herbal and slightly nutty. Crumble them as they cool.

When the roots are done, arrange them on a warm plate. Drizzle the burnt honey over, scatter the hazelnuts and crisped leaves, finish with fleur de sel and a thread of cold oil. The contrast matters: hot root, cool honey, crisp leaf, nutty weight. You should taste the carrot first—its mineral sweetness, its aromatic spine—then everything else moves around it.

## Wild Cherry Leather with Burnt Honey and Black Garlic

# Wild Cherry Leather with Burnt Honey and Black Garlic

Wild cherries are one of those fruits that remind you why flavour matters more than fashion. Sweet, never acid, they have a mineral depth that distinguishes them utterly from the cultivated versions. They're small, they're work — each one has a stone the size of a worry — but the flesh carries a subtle bitterness underneath the sugar that makes them sing in concentration. This is cherry leather: fruit reduced to its essence, folded through with aged sweetness and umami silt.

**Ingredients**

2kg wild cherries (Prunus avium), stoned  
100g honey  
30g black garlic, finely minced  
Fleur de sel  
1 small pinch of cayenne  
A squeeze of aged vinegar (sherry or red wine)

**Method**

Stone your cherries over a bowl to catch the juice — don't waste it. You're looking at a few hours of patient work, but it's the kind of work that settles you. Put the fruit and its collected juice into a wide, heavy pan and set it over a low flame. Don't rush this. You're not cooking cherries; you're concentrating them. Stir occasionally with a wooden spoon, breaking down the fruit as it softens. After an hour or so, when the volume has dropped by half and the colour has deepened to wine, taste it. That's when you'll understand what wild cherry actually is.

Meanwhile, warm your honey gently — don't boil it — and let it sit in a small pan until it darkens to amber at the edges. That burnt note will anchor everything. Fold the black garlic through while it's still warm and set it aside.

When your cherry reduction has collapsed to about 800g and coats the spoon with a slight drag, remove it from the heat and fold through the burnt honey and black garlic carefully. Season with fleur de sel — a pinch, maybe two — and a small squeeze of vinegar to lift the whole thing. The acidity isn't from the fruit; it comes from this finish, which is how you know it's right.

Spread the leather onto parchment paper in a rectangle about the thickness of a coin, no thicker. Dry it in a very low oven — 60°C is ideal — for 6 to 8 hours, until it peels cleanly from the paper but still holds some give. It should never be brittle. Cut into strips, dust with fleur de sel if you want, and wrap in paper.

**Pairing** Slice it thin and serve alongside aged cheese — something nutty and dense, like a mature Gruyère or a hard goat cheese. The mineral cherry and the burnt sweetness belong to that conversation.

## Wood Avens Root Tea with Roasted Roots, Toasted Grains, and Honey

# Wood Avens Root Tea with Roasted Roots, Toasted Grains, and Honey

Wood avens root tastes like someone whispered clove and cinnamon into a piece of fresh, lightly bitter earth. It's a plant that wants to warm you — the medieval herbalists knew what they were doing when they called it blessed herb. I use it as a spice root rather than as a medicinal tincture, which means treating it with the same respect you'd give to a piece of old ginger or dried turmeric. Roasted, it becomes mineral and sweet; steeped, it carries gentleness and complexity. This is a winter drink, something to make when the light is thin and you want substance without weight.

---

## Ingredients

For 4 small cups:

- 8–10 fresh wood avens roots, well scrubbed (collected in spring if possible, or autumn; avoid digging in summer when the plant flowers)
- 2 tablespoons rye grain, toasted in a dry pan until fragrant
- 1 tablespoon barley, toasted the same way
- 1 small piece of dried orange peel
- 1 dried bay leaf
- 3 tablespoons good honey
- A pinch of sea salt
- 1 litre of cold water

---

## Method

Start by roasting your wood avens roots. Slice them lengthwise so they're not too thick — about a pencil's width — and lay them on a baking tray. Put them in a low oven, around 120°C, for 40 minutes. You're not trying to char them; you want them to dehydrate slightly and toast just enough to release their oils. They'll smell like clove and something deeper, almost leather-like. This is the point where the magic happens.

While they're roasting, toast your grains separately in a dry frying pan over medium heat. Rye and barley together give you earthiness without heaviness — something grainy and substantial that sits well with the root's spice. Listen for the smell to shift from grain to something nuttier and warmer. About three minutes should do it.

Once the roots are ready, crack them slightly with the side of your knife — not pulverizing, just encouraging them to release more freely. Put the roasted roots, toasted grains, orange peel, and bay leaf into a large pot with the cold water and a pinch of salt. Bring it gently to a simmer and let it go for 20 minutes. Don't boil it hard — simmering is softer and more respectful of what the plant wants to give you. The colour will deepen towards gold and amber.

Pour through a fine sieve. Stir in the honey while it's still warm — it dissolves better that way and tastes less forced. Taste it. It should feel warming without being hot, spiced without being aggressive, with a gentle bitterness underneath that reminds you this comes from the earth.

Serve in small cups, the kind you'd use for something ceremonial rather than casual. Drink it slowly.

---

**Variation:** If you want something with more body, add a tablespoon of ground almonds or a splash of oat milk after straining. In summer, when the roots are younger and less available, use the young leaves instead — simmer them for just 8 minutes and steep a small piece of root alongside for depth. The drink becomes brighter, more herbaceous, less warming — but still itself.

## Wood Violet Broth with Spring Barley and Wild Greens

# Wood Violet Broth with Spring Barley and Wild Greens

**Viola riviniana** — the common dog-violet — earns its place here not as decoration but as the structural backbone of a broth. The young leaves carry a mild, green mucilaginous quality that thickens liquid naturally as they cook, giving this soup a body that feels almost silky without any flour or starch involved. The raw flowers and buds go in at the end, cool and faintly sweet, doing what only delicate things can — arriving last and saying everything.

---

**Serves 4**

Young dog-violet leaves and buds — a good generous double handful, gathered from woodland edge or hedgebank, before the plant gets going fully into flower
Dog-violet flowers — a loose handful, to finish
Pearl barley — 150g
White onion — 1 medium, finely diced
Celery — 2 sticks, sliced
Garlic — 3 cloves, crushed
Good olive oil
Vegetable stock — 1.2 litres, hot and well-seasoned
Lemon — half, juice only
Sea salt and black pepper
Flat-leaf parsley — small handful, roughly torn
Optional: a few wild garlic leaves if the season overlaps, shredded fine

---

**Method**

Start by getting the barley into the stock early. Rinse it well, add it to the hot stock, and let it cook gently at a low simmer — it needs around 35 to 40 minutes to become tender but not collapsed. You want it to still have something to say when you bite it.

While that's underway, take a heavy pan and warm a generous pour of olive oil over a medium heat. Add the onion and celery and cook them down slowly — not rushing, not browning — just softening and sweetening, maybe 10 minutes. Add the garlic and give it another 3 minutes. Season here, properly, so the base carries flavour from the start.

When the barley is nearly done — tender, just holding its shape — ladle the whole lot into the pan with the onion base and bring it together. Let it simmer for another 5 minutes as one thing. Now add the violet leaves and buds. You'll notice almost immediately that the broth begins to take on a slightly fuller, more rounded consistency as they release their natural thickening quality into the liquid. This is the okra-like behaviour PFAF references, and it's real — subtle but genuinely there. Cook for another 4 or 5 minutes only. You don't want those leaves to go grey and lifeless.

Squeeze in the lemon juice, taste, adjust the salt. Add the parsley. Wild garlic if you have it.

Ladle into wide bowls — deep enough to hold heat, wide enough to see the surface properly. Then scatter the fresh violet flowers across the top. They'll sit on the surface of the broth, vivid and cool against the warmth beneath. Don't stir them in. Let them be what they are.

---

**One suggestion:** If you're cooking this in late spring and you can find wood sorrel growing nearby, a few leaves torn and placed alongside the violet flowers at the end adds a bright, citrus-sharp note that cuts through the body of the broth beautifully. The two grow in similar habitats for good reason — they belong together on the plate.

## Yarrow-Cured Goat's Cheese with Roasted Beetroot, Toasted Walnuts, and Wildflower Honey

# Yarrow-Cured Goat's Cheese with Roasted Beetroot, Toasted Walnuts, and Wildflower Honey

---

Yarrow is a serious herb — bitter, aromatic, almost medicinal in its intensity — and that bitterness is exactly why it belongs in a dish built around sweetness and fat. The young leaves have a complexity that makes most cultivated herbs look polite. Used with restraint and respect, yarrow doesn't fade into the background; it threads itself through everything it touches.

---

**Serves 4 as a starter or light lunch**

---

200g firm goat's cheese log, sliced into rounds approximately 1.5cm thick

A good handful of young yarrow leaves, washed — perhaps 15 to 20 leaves, more if they're small

4 medium beetroots, mixed colours if you can find them

80g walnut halves

2 tablespoons wildflower honey, plus a little more to finish

2 tablespoons good olive oil

1 tablespoon apple cider vinegar — raw and live if possible

Flaky sea salt and black pepper

A few yarrow flowers if they're in season — these are not decoration, they carry flavour too

Optional: a small handful of rocket or watercress to extend the plate

---

**Method**

Start with the beetroots. Wrap them whole and unpeeled in foil with a little salt and a splash of water, then roast at 200°C for 45 minutes to an hour until a skewer passes through without resistance. You want them collapsing slightly at the edges, caramelised in their own sugars. Let them cool until you can handle them, then peel — the skins will come away easily in your hands. Cut into uneven wedges. Don't be precise about it. The irregularity holds dressing better than clean geometry.

While the beetroots are in the oven, make the yarrow cure. Take roughly half your yarrow leaves and chop them fairly fine — not a dust, more a rough mince. Combine with a teaspoon of flaky salt and a teaspoon of honey, mix it together until it becomes a loose, fragrant paste. The bitterness of the yarrow softens slightly when it meets the honey, but it doesn't disappear — that tension is the point. Press each goat's cheese round into this mixture, coating both flat faces, then lay them on a plate and leave them in the fridge for at least two hours. Overnight is better. The cure draws out a little moisture, firms the surface, and the yarrow begins to permeate the cheese rather than just sitting on top of it.

Toast the walnuts in a dry pan over a medium heat, shaking regularly, until they smell roasted and the skins are beginning to crack and darken in places. Take them off the heat, let them cool, then break them loosely in your hands. You want texture here, not crumbs.

For the dressing, whisk together the olive oil, apple cider vinegar, remaining honey, a pinch of salt, and a small amount of pepper. Taste it — it should be sweet but with enough acid to cut through the goat's cheese and the earthiness of the beet. Finely chop the remaining fresh yarrow leaves and stir them in. Dress the beetroot wedges while they're still slightly warm if you can — warm vegetables drink dressing in a way that cold ones don't.

To plate, lay the dressed beetroot across the base of each plate in a generous, slightly haphazard arrangement. Rest one or two rounds of cured goat's cheese on top. Scatter over the broken walnuts, then finish with a slow drizzle of honey directly from the spoon. If you have yarrow flowers, pull the tiny florets free from the stem and scatter them now — they carry the same aromatic quality as the leaves but with a gentler edge. A last pinch of flaky salt across everything.

# Mugwort (Artemisia vulgaris)

## Mugwort Mochi with Brown Butter, Sea Salt & Crisp Sage

# Mugwort Mochi with Brown Butter, Sea Salt & Crisp Sage

Mugwort works here because it's bitter without cruelty — that grassy, slightly metallic edge cuts through rich carbohydrate and fat in a way that feels necessary rather than decorative. The young spring leaves have enough body to colour and flavour the dough without turning it grey or murky. You're using it structurally, the way the Japanese do, not as an afterthought.

**For the mochi (makes about 24 pieces)**

400g glutinous rice flour  
250ml water  
100g young mugwort leaves, blanched and finely chopped  
½ teaspoon fleur de sel  
50g cornstarch for dusting  

**For serving**

80g good butter  
12–15 fresh sage leaves  
Fleur de sel  
Cracked black pepper  

---

**Method**

Blanch your mugwort leaves in salted boiling water for ninety seconds — just enough to kill any bitterness and lock in the colour. Drain them properly, squeeze them dry with your hands, then chop them fine. This matters: wet dough is slack dough.

Mix your rice flour with the salt. Pour the water in slowly, stirring with a wooden spoon until you have a rough, shaggy mass. Knead it for two minutes — the gluten network is thin in glutinous rice, so you're looking for coherence, not elasticity. Fold in your mugwort until the colour is even throughout. The dough should be soft, warm, and slightly tacky.

Dust your work surface with cornstarch. Tear off a piece of dough about the size of a walnut, roll it gently between your palms into a ball, then flatten it slightly with your thumb. You're aiming for something between a coin and a quail's egg. Set them on the cornstarch-dusted surface as you go.

Bring a large pot of water to a rolling boil. Drop the mochi in a few at a time — don't crowd the pan. They'll sink, then float. Give them thirty seconds after they float, then lift them out with a slotted spoon onto a clean cloth.

While they're still warm, melt your butter in a heavy pan over medium heat. Let it foam and turn golden — about three minutes. Add the sage leaves and listen for the crackle. It's a small gesture, but it matters. Toss in your warm mochi gently, turn them once or twice so they catch the butter and sage, then plate them immediately with a generous pinch of salt and pepper.

Eat them within a minute or two. They're best when the outside is still yielding and the warmth carries the bitterness of the mugwort and the sweetness of the butter into each other.

---

**Variation**  
In winter, when fresh mugwort is scarce, dry the leaves in summer, steep a teaspoon in hot water for five minutes, reduce it to a concentrated liquor, and whisk that into the dough instead. You'll get less colour, but the flavour comes through cleaner — more like the original Japanese intention.

## Nettle, Preserved Lemon & Smoked Almond Soup with Spiced Yoghurt

# Nettle, Preserved Lemon & Smoked Almond Soup with Spiced Yoghurt

Nettles carry a depth that spinach never quite reaches — something earthier, more mineral, with a faint savouriness that calls for fat and acid to frame it properly. Gathered in early spring when the shoots are still tight and dark, before the plant has any idea what it's about to become, they're as vital as anything you'll find in a market. This soup is built around that vitality.

---

**Serves 4**

**For the soup**

- 400g young nettle tops, picked with gloves, washed
- 2 medium white onions, finely sliced
- 4 cloves garlic, crushed
- 1 large floury potato, peeled and diced (roughly 250g)
- 1 preserved lemon, pith removed, skin finely chopped
- 1.2 litres good vegetable stock — ideally one with some body to it
- 3 tbsp olive oil
- Sea salt and black pepper

**For the smoked almonds**

- 80g blanched almonds
- 1 tsp smoked paprika
- ½ tsp cumin, freshly ground
- 1 tbsp olive oil
- Flaked sea salt

**For the spiced yoghurt**

- 200g full-fat natural yoghurt
- 1 tsp ground coriander
- ½ tsp dried chilli flakes
- Zest of half a lemon
- A small handful of fresh mint, finely chopped
- Salt to taste

---

**Method**

Start with the almonds. Toss them with the oil, paprika, cumin, and a good pinch of flaked salt, then lay them out on a tray and roast at 180°C for ten to twelve minutes until they're deep gold and the spices have caught slightly. They should smell almost smoky. Set them aside and resist eating them before the soup is done.

For the yoghurt — just combine everything in a bowl, taste it, adjust the salt, and leave it alone in the fridge to let the flavours settle into each other.

Now the soup. Get a heavy-bottomed pot warm over a medium heat, pour in the olive oil, and add your onions with a generous pinch of salt. You want them to soften slowly — give them a good fifteen minutes, stirring now and then, until they're translucent and beginning to take on a little sweetness. Add the garlic and the preserved lemon and cook for another two or three minutes. The lemon will bloom in the heat and that brininess will anchor everything that follows.

Add the potato and stir it through the onions so it picks up some of that flavour, then pour in the stock. Bring it to a steady simmer and cook until the potato is completely soft — about fifteen minutes. You want it to dissolve into the soup when blended, giving it body without heaviness.

Now the nettles. Drop them into the pot — they'll be voluminous at first, but they'll collapse within thirty seconds. Once they're fully wilted, take the pot off the heat immediately. Speed matters here. The longer they sit in the heat, the more that green vitality starts to dull. Blend the soup straight away, either in a jug blender in batches or with a stick blender right in the pot. Blend it properly — this should be silky, not rustic. Taste it. Adjust the salt. If it feels flat, a little more preserved lemon will sharpen it without making it sharp, if that makes sense. You'll know when it's right.

Ladle it into warm bowls. Spoon the yoghurt into the centre — a proper generous spoonful, not a timid swirl. Scatter the smoked almonds over the top, rough-chop a few if you like

## Nettle, White Miso & Toasted Hazelnut Soup

# Nettle, White Miso & Toasted Hazelnut Soup

Nettles have a flavour that sits somewhere between spinach and the sea — deep, mineral, faintly grassy — and that iron-rich vitality responds beautifully to fermented depth and fat. This is a soup built around that quality: white miso to amplify the umami, toasted hazelnuts for body and sweetness, and a finish of good oil to carry it all to the bowl. Spring nettles only — tops of young plants, before they've thought about flowering.

---

**Serves 4**

400g young nettle tops (top 4–6 leaves, picked with gloves)
1 large white onion, sliced
3 cloves garlic, crushed
1 large floury potato, roughly diced
2 tbsp white miso paste
1 litre good vegetable stock — ideally something you've made yourself
80g blanched hazelnuts, toasted in a dry pan until golden
Zest of half a lemon
50ml whole milk or oat milk
Cold-pressed rapeseed oil or good olive oil, to finish
Sea salt and black pepper
Optional: a few drops of cider vinegar at the end

---

**Method**

Start with the hazelnuts. Get a dry pan hot and toast them until they colour properly — not blond, properly golden with some dark patches. You'll smell when they're there. Set them aside.

In a heavy-bottomed pot, warm a generous pour of oil over a medium heat and soften the onion slowly. Give it ten minutes at least. You're not rushing here — you want sweetness, not colour. Add the garlic and the potato and let them get acquainted with the heat for another few minutes.

Pour in the stock, bring to a simmer, and cook until the potato is completely tender, around 15 minutes. While that's happening, blanch the nettles in a separate pan of well-salted boiling water for two minutes — this kills the sting and sets that extraordinary green. Drain them, squeeze out the excess water, and drop them into the soup pot.

Add the miso now, stirring it in off direct heat so it doesn't lose its character. Then most of the hazelnuts — keep a handful back — along with the lemon zest and the milk. Blend the whole thing until very smooth. This soup deserves a proper blend: you want silk, not texture. Taste it. Adjust salt, and if it needs lifting, a few drops of cider vinegar will do it cleanly.

Ladle into warm bowls, finish with the reserved hazelnuts broken roughly in your hands, a thin thread of cold-pressed rapeseed oil spiralled across the surface, and nothing else.

---

**To vary it:** A spoonful of crème fraîche stirred through just before serving adds richness and a gentle acidity that plays well with the mineral depth of the nettles. Or go further into fermentation — a few slices of quick-pickled green chilli across the top will give the whole thing a sharp, lively edge that makes you eat it faster.

## Stone Age Nettle Pudding

**Stone Age Nettle Pudding**

Britain's oldest recorded recipe, dated to c. 6000 BC and possibly 8,000 years old. Reconstructed by Dr Ruth Fairchild at the University of Wales Institute (Cardiff Met) in 2007 from archaeological evidence, social history texts, and experimental archaeology. A savoury dumpling — "pudding" in its original sense — boiled inside a muslin cloth alongside venison or wild boar.

**Serves:** 4
**Prep time:** 20 minutes
**Cook time:** 2 hours (or until meat is done)

---

**Ingredients**

- 2 bunches young nettle leaves (*Urtica dioica*)
- 1 bunch [sorrel](/species?s=Rumex+acetosa) (*Rumex acetosa*)
- 1 bunch [watercress](/species?s=Nasturtium+officinale) (*Nasturtium officinale*)
- 1 bunch [dandelion](/species?s=Taraxacum+officinale) leaves (*Taraxacum officinale*)
- Small bunch chives
- 1 cup barley flour
- 1 tsp sea salt
- Water to bind
- Venison or wild boar joint for the pot (pork joint as modern substitute)

---

**Method**

1. Harvest young nettle tips wearing gloves. Rinse well.
2. Finely chop all foraged greens — nettles, sorrel, watercress, dandelion, chives.
3. Mix chopped greens with barley flour and salt. Add water gradually until the mixture binds into a rough dough — firm enough to hold shape, not wet.
4. Place the dough in the centre of a linen or muslin cloth. Tie securely, leaving enough string to retrieve the pudding from the pot.
5. Add the cloth-wrapped pudding to a pot of simmering venison or wild boar. The pudding cooks alongside the meat, absorbing the savoury stock.
6. Cook for at least 2 hours, ensuring the pudding stays submerged. Top up water as needed.
7. Remove the pudding, leave to cool slightly, then unwrap the cloth and cut into thick slices.
8. Serve with chunks of barley bread and the braised meat.

---

**Notes**

Boiling neutralises the sting completely. The pudding can also be served standalone without the meat. Modern variations use oats instead of barley, and some foragers chill and pan-fry the sliced pudding into crispy patties. The companion greens — sorrel, watercress, dandelion — would all have been gathered from the same spring hedgerow.
